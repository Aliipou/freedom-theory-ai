## Introduction: A Journey to the Depths of the Meaning of Life

### Chapter of Acquaintance: A Journey to the Depths of Freedom, Religion, Iran, and the Future of Humanity

This book does not begin with a simple question; it begins with a historical wound. A wound that has for centuries been lodged in the body of Iran, religion, freedom, property, reason, and humanity. A wound that has sometimes been called tyranny, sometimes backwardness, sometimes poverty, sometimes the distortion of religion, sometimes Westernization, sometimes Easternization, sometimes a crisis of the state, sometimes a crisis of meaning, and sometimes the bitter fate of a nation.

But in this book I want to show that the root of many of these scattered names lies in a deeper issue: **the loss of the meaning of freedom.**

Throughout history, freedom has been one of the most used — yet most ambiguous — words of humanity. Everyone has spoken of freedom: states, revolutionaries, philosophers, mystics, intellectuals, kings, parties, schools of thought, empires, and even oppressors. But whenever freedom lacks an objective, assessable definition, the very word that should save humanity becomes an instrument of slavery. Power first defines freedom, then imposes that very definition on humanity, and finally calls slavery freedom.

This book begins from this very point: **freedom must emerge from ambiguity.** Freedom must be definable, measurable, agreeable, formalizeable, and defensible. Freedom must not remain a poetic, emotional, mystical, political, or propagandistic state. Freedom must become something that: if violated, can be demonstrated; if preserved, can be explained; and if someone destroys freedom in the name of freedom, they can be stopped.

My answer in this book is clear: **Freedom, in its objective form, is nothing other than individual property rights.**

But by property I do not mean only property over a house, land, money, or goods. Property begins with the human body — with a person's life, will, time, labor, thought, honor, family, contract, achievement, land, sustenance, choice, and responsibility. A person who does not own their body is not free. A person who does not own the product of their labor is not free. A person who has no right to defend their assets, family, speech, belief, contract, and will is not free. A person who must receive their sustenance, security, meaning, and law from the state is on the path to slavery — even if this slavery is called justice, security, patriotism, or piety.

Here the question of religion opens up again.

In this book I do not want to separate religion from freedom; on the contrary, I want to show that true religion — if cleansed of mysticism, statism, superstition, anti-rational philosophies, and power-centered interpretations — is the most precise system for defending freedom. Religion, in its essence, did not come to make humanity a slave of the state; it did not come to demean human property; it did not come to portray the material world, the body, the earth, the market, the family, and cultivation as worthless. **Religion came to free humanity from slavery to other than God.**

Islam, in this reading, is not merely an identity label or a collection of rituals. Islam means submission before God; and submission before God, at the social level, means submission before the rights that God has placed for His servants. *Salam* (peace) is not merely a verbal greeting; *salam* is the proclamation of property-based peace: I will not transgress against your life, property, body, will, honor, and ownership — and you accept this same covenant. The answer in this book has one key word: **mysticism** (*irfan*).

Mysticism sometimes comes in the guise of religion, sometimes philosophy, sometimes intellectualism, sometimes socialism, sometimes nationalism, sometimes the modern state, and sometimes even the language of spirituality and ethics.

The criterion for identifying it is simple: wherever human property, human freedom, human free will, and human right over one's body, property, honor, and life is demeaned, we are faced with a form of anti-freedom mysticism. Wherever the material world is considered worthless, the earth is seen as base, the body is humiliated, prosperity and market and cultivation are presented as low matters, or the state takes the place of God — mysticism is at work. Idolatry has not disappeared; it has merely found new forms.

In this book I want to show that humanity's main conflict is not the conflict between religion and irreligion; it is the conflict between **religion and mysticism.** Between revelation and the fabrication of revelation. Between property and confiscation. Between freedom and the state. Between humanity as God's vicegerent on earth, and humanity as a slave of power.

## Packing Up: A Summary Framing of the Journey

Line by line, this book contains earth-shaking statements that will shock you at first, then you will digest them. Therefore, for every topic and for the entire book, patience is continuously needed.

To understand what we are about to arrive at in this book, we must first know what all of us needed: **Freedom!** But this single word has been the subject of much contention throughout history. Why?

Because everyone states their own definition of freedom. Freedom in isolation has neither controversy nor dispute — it is in human community that it matters to know what freedom means. When the discussion turns to human society, there are only two paths before us: either by the law of force, one person's or a limited group's definition of freedom must be determined for others (which naturally, when some humans determine and compel the definition of freedom for other humans, they are in practice theorizing slavery for them); or human beings must arrive at an agreement and social contract among themselves regarding the definition of freedom.

For humans to be able to "agree" on a subject or definition or anything, that subject must be "agreeable." What things are agreeable?

For example, can all humans agree on the deliciousness of a food? Certainly not. Because each person has a different understanding of taste. The deliciousness or tastelessness of food is for each person a unique experience that cannot even be transferred to another. I cannot transfer the pleasure I experience from the taste of a food — even one you also like — to you, because it is a mental and personal experience. In fact, the taste of food is a *subjective* matter.

But when we tell someone "bring me a glass of water," that person brings an object whose depth exceeds its width, and whose width is such that an ordinary human hand can grip it from around it. It may be glass or ceramic or plastic. It may be handled or handleless. But the word "glass" in both our minds has a range of meaning and concept that can be defined and described precisely in words and sentences. A glass and the definition of a glass are an *objective* matter. We have been able to agree that the word "glass" applies to something with specific characteristics that are the same in both our minds.

In fact, **for a definition or something to be agreeable, it needs to be objective** — meaning it must be the same thing across the inter-subjective space of individuals. Like a glass, and unlike the taste of food.

Therefore: we must do one fundamental thing — provide a definition of freedom that is objective, expressable in human language (Persian, English, Arabic, machine languages like Python, C...) and capable of closing its door to additional interpretations. Like the definition of a glass.

Human languages and machine languages are all subsets of a larger set called **formal languages.** A formal language has had specific forms from the beginning of history — from when drawings were made on cave walls — all the way to today's various languages of different civilizations and various machine languages. Formal languages are mutually convertible and their semantic and grammatical correspondences can be shown.

Now that we understand what a formal language is (a set of letters, strings, and forms that are placed alongside each other with specific formulas, rules, and grammar), for us to be able to define rules that allow us to describe and derive inferences from objective phenomena, we need a **formal system.** Formal systems are subsets of formal languages that have rules for inference, argumentation, and proof.

When we say "freedom is my right," in fact we must be able to:
1. Define freedom objectively, expressed in formal language, free from ambiguity and additional interpretation, identical in our inter-subjective space.
2. Place it as a right and an axiom.
3. Build a formal system for it with logical inference, argumentation, and proof — one that has common principles agreeable to everyone, comprehensible and unique, so that two people cannot draw two different interpretations from it.
4. This formal system, which has agreed-upon axiomatic principles between everyone, must not be internally contradictory.

**Conclusion: We will have only "one coherent axiomatic formal system for freedom" — and we must find it.**

The entire book before you is a journey to find this coherent axiomatic formal system. The whole of the present book in front of you is this journey.

**This is the foundational monetary step:**

- Legalize holding and trading foreign currencies, gold, silver, cryptocurrencies
- Allow multi-currency contracts
- Allow individuals and firms to keep accounts in the currency of their choice
- Allow private banks and institutions (with sufficient reserves, clear redemption commitments, transparent auditing, insurance, and legal liability) to issue private currency and credit
- The rial and the central bank remain but lose their monopoly
- The central bank goes from being the god of money to being one player among many in the money market

**The key principle:** No money becomes money by force. Money must earn trust. Bad money only survives when people are imprisoned within it. Currency freedom means people have the right to exit bad money.

**Zero structural inflation means:** No human institution can seize past labor and purchasing power from people without their consent by manipulating money and credit.

### Step 7: Clarify Energy Ownership Before Liberalizing Energy Prices

Iran's energy problem must not begin with: "How much should petrol cost?" Both sides of that debate — "keep it cheap" and "raise the price" — presuppose that the state owns, prices, and controls energy. That presupposition is wrong.

**The Theory of Freedom's approach:** Every living citizen has an equal share in communal energy resources. If they consume their share, they have used it. If they consume less, they have the right to sell their unused share, store it, transfer it, or use it in contracts. The market in energy shares produces the actual price.

This transforms the problem: instead of "the state is raising prices on people," the situation becomes "people are trading their own property." The poor, low-consuming citizen becomes the first beneficiary — they sell their unused share.

### Step 8: Clarify Land and Spatial Commons Ownership

Land is not state property. Land that the state acquired through confiscation, nationalization, or "public interest" seizures must be returned to property owners or transferred to citizens through transparent, rule-based mechanisms.

The key principle: **Urban and rural commons should be managed as shared property under clear contractual rules, not as state property under bureaucratic discretion.**

### Step 9: Clarify Ownership of State Enterprises, Quasi-State Entities

State companies must be transferred to real private ownership — not to quasi-governmental entities, not to connected persons, not to pension funds controlled by the state, but to genuine private owners.

The principle of transfer: Employees first, then citizens through transparent mechanisms. Managers get conditional, restricted shares with accountability structures. Quasi-state ownership (*khosoulati*) is prohibited.

### Step 10: Resolve Insolvent Banks and Pension Funds

Insolvent banks must not be saved by printing money (inflation tax on all citizens). Their assets must be liquidated in order: managers and major shareholders bear losses first, then larger depositors, then through other mechanisms. Small depositors must be protected to the maximum possible extent through transparent mechanisms.

### Step 11: Remove Anti-Production Taxes

Replace complex, production-discouraging taxes with narrow commons fees:
- Land value tax on location value (not improvements)
- User fees for commons (roads, utilities, registration services)
- No income tax
- No value-added tax on productive activity
- No corporate tax that discourages investment and production

### Step 12: Immediately Remove Tariffs or Reduce to a Single Low Number

Tariffs are a tax on consumers to protect rentier producers. Under sanctions, they additionally funnel trade through connected monopolists. Remove them in one step or reduce to a very low, fixed number. Completely free foreign trade.

### Step 13: Break the Monopoly on Sanctions Circumvention

Sanctions circumvention must not be a monopoly of the state, security institutions, special companies, special banks, favored importers, or those close to power. If trade must occur under difficult conditions, the more competitive the field, the lower the cost, the less corruption, and the greater the chance of success.

### Step 14: Liberate Education, Health, Science, and Religion from the State

**Education:** The state has no right to consider the child's mind its property. The family, not the state, is the primary responsible party for a child's upbringing. Schools should be contractual relationships between families, teachers, managers, students, and local communities.

**Health:** The state has no right to place the human body under bureaucratic guardianship in the name of health. Patients are parties to contracts, not subjects of ministries.

**Science:** Universities must gain ownership independence, financial independence, educational independence, research independence, and contractual independence. State monopoly on credentials must end.

**Religion:** If religion becomes statist, it transforms from a formal system defending freedom and property rights into a tool of guardianship, compulsion, budgets, appointments, ideology, social control, and the justification of power. Religion's statification is religion's corruption. **Religious freedom means religion returns to its true place: defending freedom, property rights, responsibility, family, honesty, faithfulness to covenants, and the rejection of slavery to other than God.**

### Step 15: Two-Stage Downsizing of the State

**Stage 1:** Stop new interventions immediately.

**Stage 2:** Structural withdrawal from proprietorial activities to adjudicatory activities. The state must withdraw from:
- Money, banking monopoly, price controls, energy, land, housing, foreign trade, imports and exports, education, health, universities, science, religion, culture, media, production, agriculture, industry, mining, transport, professional licensing, tariff-setting, lifestyle definition, and intervention in the family.

**What the state must be limited to:**
- External defense
- Judgment and dispute resolution
- Registration and protection of property rights
- Contract enforcement
- Security of persons and property
- Border protection
- Minimal, transparent, limited, temporary support for those truly unable to support themselves

**A state limited against its people can be strong against its enemies. A large state against its people weakens society and makes the country fragile.**
